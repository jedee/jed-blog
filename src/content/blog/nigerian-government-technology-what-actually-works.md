The gap between policy and execution in Nigerian government tech is not primarily about funding. It's about institutional capacity, vendor management, and the absence of engineers who speak both languages.

<!--more-->

---

## The Policy Is Always Right

Nigeria has a National Digital Economy Policy and Strategy. There are digital transformation committees at federal level. There are mandates for e-government services, electronic procurement, digital identity. The policy documents are thorough, well-structured, and in many cases, genuinely visionary.

The implementation tells a different story.

I have spent two years working inside and adjacent to Nigerian government technology projects — as a contractor, a consultant, and in one case, as the person who had to explain why the biometric attendance system the ministry bought three years ago had never been switched on.

Here is what I have observed.

---

## The Vendor Problem

Government technology procurement in Nigeria follows a predictable pattern:

1. A committee is formed
2. The committee writes a Request for Proposal
3. The RFP is sent to a shortlist of vendors, most of whom are not technology companies but *government relations companies* that happened to register a tech subsidiary
4. The vendor with the best relationship wins
5. The vendor builds something
6. The vendor trains nobody
7. The system is launched
8. The system is abandoned within 18 months

The fundamental problem: **governments buy technology the way they buy construction** — as a deliverable, not as a service.

You do not hire a construction company to build a road and then never maintain it. But in government tech procurement, this is the default. The contract specifies what gets built. It rarely specifies what happens when it breaks. It almost never specifies who is responsible for training, change management, and user adoption.

---

## The Capacity Problem

There is a genuine shortage of IT capacity at the state and local government level. Not just technical capacity — institutional capacity.

What do I mean by institutional capacity?

- The ability to specify what you actually need, as opposed to what you think a vendor wants to hear
- The ability to evaluate whether a delivered system meets the specification
- The ability to operate and maintain the system after the vendor leaves
- The ability to extract value from the data the system generates

These are not technical problems. They are organizational problems. And they are not solved by hiring more IT staff — they are solved by building IT *culture*, which takes longer than any single government's term.

I worked with one ministry that had spent ₦40 million on a document management system. Three years later, it had never been adopted internally. When I asked why, the answer was revealing: *"We don't have anyone who knows what it's supposed to replace."*

The system had been specified by a committee, approved by management, and implemented by a vendor. No one had thought to ask the people doing the actual work what their current process was, what was painful about it, and what a digital version would need to do differently.

---

## The Language Problem

The most underestimated gap in Nigerian government tech: **engineers do not speak civil servant, and civil servants do not speak engineer.**

A civil servant says *"We need a system that tracks our staff attendance"* and means: something that helps them know who came to work, something they can show to the director when asked, something that makes the monthly HR report less painful to compile.

An engineer hears *"We need a biometric attendance system with real-time clock synchronization, role-based access control, and automated reporting"* and builds exactly that — technically correct, completely unusable.

The engineer designed for the stated requirement. The civil servant needed something that fit their actual workflow.

This is not unique to Nigeria. But it is particularly acute here because the gap between technical literacy at decision-making level and technical literacy at implementation level is enormous. And the person who signs the acceptance test is often several layers away from the person who will actually use the system.

---

## What Actually Works

The government technology projects I have seen succeed have three things in common:

**1. A named internal champion with authority**

Not a committee. Not a unit. A person — usually senior, usually not from IT — who has decided this project will succeed and who has the political capital to make it happen. This person is the bridge between the technical delivery and the organizational change.

**2. Technology that fits existing workflows**

Not technology that *replaces* the workflow — technology that fits *into* it. The biometric system I built for AMML works because it replaced a paper register with a digital equivalent, not because it required market supervisors to learn a new way of thinking about attendance. The export-via-USB approach was not technically optimal. It was culturally appropriate.

**3. Vendor contracts that include adoption, not just delivery**

The best government tech contract I have ever seen specified: system delivery (6 months), user acceptance testing (2 months), training rollout (3 months), and post-launch support (12 months). It also specified that the vendor had to hire a *government liaison* — someone whose job was to sit with civil servants and help them use the system in their actual working context.

That project is still running three years later. Most do not make it past 18 months.

---

## The Opportunity

Despite everything, there is genuine opportunity in Nigerian government tech.

The National Identity Number ecosystem is maturing. The cashless economy infrastructure is in place. The JAMB model — rigid, bureaucratic, deeply frustrating to users — demonstrates that government can build systems that handle enormous scale when the political will exists.

What is missing is not funding or policy. It is the engineering culture to build for *users*, not for *specifications*.

That means:
- User research before system design
- Gradual rollout instead of big-bang launches
- Post-launch support as a contractual requirement, not an afterthought
- Internal capacity building, not just vendor-delivered training

The engineers who will fix Nigerian government tech are not going to come from abroad. They are going to be Nigerian engineers who understand both the technical problem and the institutional context — and who are willing to spend time in the ministry, not just at the keyboard.
